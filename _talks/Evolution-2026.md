The molecular chaperone Hsp90 is a classic example of an evolutionary capacitor, yet its role in shaping major evolutionary transitions remains poorly resolved. Here we use long-term experimental evolution in snowflake yeast to test how changes in Hsp90 expression influence the emergence of macroscopic multicellularity. In the Multicellularity Long-Term Evolution Experiment (MuLTEE), simple multicellular clusters evolved to become >20,000-fold larger through cellular elongation and physical entanglement, and this transition was repeatedly associated with convergent downregulation of Hsp90. To directly examine Hsp90’s role, we performed an evolutionary replay experiment comparing a standard background to a strain overexpressing HSC82, the major yeast Hsp90 homolog. Although Hsc82 overexpression initially reduced fitness and cluster size, all five overexpression lineages convergently evolved macroscopic multicellularity within 100 transfers, considerably faster than in the original MuLTEE. Overexpression was subsequently lost, yet the macroscopic phenotype persisted, indicating that a transient proteostatic perturbation can durably redirect evolutionary outcomes. Genome sequencing revealed that transient Hsc82 overexpression reshaped the mutational landscape, consistent with Hsc82 buffering client mutations from purifying selection and enabling alternative adaptive routes. Persistent mutations in Hsc82-interacting genes, including STT4, synergized with elevated Hsc82 expression to increase cell and cluster size, while recurrent aneuploidy repeatedly arose and reverted, providing a reversible mechanism for tuning phenotypic state. Together, these results demonstrate that transient disruption of a central proteostatic regulator can accelerate a major evolutionary transition and open new evolutionary paths to macroscopic multicellularity.
